# Paradox

## Identity

Paradox is the sword made from ruined Antithesis at the end of Book 1.

`Paradox` is the durable in-world name of the remade weapon. Maya names it.

Paradox is not a transformed form of Antithesis. Antithesis remains a dagger throughout Book 1, is ruined in the final conflict when Davian blocks Vorren's attack to give Maya and Niall respite, and is later remade into Paradox.

The high-level Vorren outcome is accepted: Maya, Niall, and Davian kill Vorren together. Exact fight choreography and the exact Antithesis-ruin beat remain deferred.

## Maker and Creation

Maya designs Paradox.

Zirene makes Paradox from the ruined Antithesis according to Maya's design.

The exact forging scene, materials, timing, and technical execution remain for later ending/outline and Zirene review.

## Physical Form

Paradox is a normal sword.

It is not accepted as a greatsword, shotel, returning weapon, or thrown weapon in the durable Book 1 object record.

## Mechanics

Paradox inherits and improves Antithesis's schema-sink/anchor role.

Its larger mass and surface area compared with Antithesis give it greater capacity to absorb, redirect, buffer, or burn off kinesis backlash. It supports Davian's kinesis more effectively than Antithesis while preserving the same broad lineage of Archeon/Zirene-derived schema-sink design.

Paradox is not bound to Davian's aura and is not explicitly bound against Arrek's aura or identity.

Returning/thrown-weapon behavior and stabilization zän remain candidate only.

## End-Book Handoff

Niall gives Paradox to Davian in the end-book courtyard scene.

The exact dialogue, emotional resolution, and whether Davian leaves or stays belong to later thread/ending/outline review, not this object record.

## Symbolism

Paradox carries the accepted symbolism of Antithesis as an inherited false-sacred tool remade into something chosen and acknowledged.

The shift from Antithesis to Paradox reflects Davian moving from knife-in-the-dark toward sword/openly chosen identity. The object embodies broken inheritance being remade rather than treated as infallible, sacred, or determinative.
