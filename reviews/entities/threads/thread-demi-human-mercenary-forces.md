# Demi-Human Mercenary Forces and Death Payments Review Packet

## Review Depth

Standard.

This thread matters for the opening attack, Maya's first real combat, Niall's killing of retreating soldiers, Harvick's possible wall conversation, the death-money economy, and the social pressure on poorer and demi-human-descended fighters. It does not need a full military-economics encyclopedia before outline work.

## Why This Entity Matters

The thread keeps attacking mercenaries from becoming faceless disposable enemies. It lets the opening battle carry class, race/lineage, and moral pressure without excusing the attack itself.

It also gives Niall's battlefield ruthlessness and Maya's later self-comparison a coherent context: killing mercenaries may be tactically defensible in war, but these are often desperate people being economically routed into lethal risk.

## Source-Backed Information

### Manuscript-Established

Current prologue material shows:

- Maya breaks the attacking force with overwhelming fire.
- She stops attacking when the soldiers break and retreat.
- Niall then cuts down retreating enemies with lightning.
- Maya screams for him to stop because they are retreating.
- Niall briefly dissociates into Alara-associated grief before returning to command posture.

Later current prose has Maya compare another killing decision to "killing attacking mercenaries who couldn't even defend themselves from me." Aldira does not give her an easy moral answer, but warns her about abrupt changes and becoming a copy of Niall.

### Planning-Established

Bullet and outline notes establish or propose:

- mercenaries are often desperate poor people willing to die so their families receive death money
- threatening to leave a wounded mercenary crippled can be worse than killing him, because it leaves him alive but unable to support his family
- Harvick may explain that mercenaries accept likely losses, try to pull back when a magus arrives, and try to redirect the fight while limiting direct exposure to magic
- the opening attack is expensive and suspicious because hiring enough people willing to take those losses costs a lot

### Accepted Context

The approved demi-human peoples record already accepts:

- mercenaries are often desperate poor people accepting lethal risk for family death payments
- mercenaries can be attackers and exploited poor people at the same time
- Durnai are common enough in armies or mercenary forces to be useful for archery/infantry roles
- detailed military organization, contract terms, and death-payment economics belong to this thread

The approved Niall record already accepts neutral event language for Niall killing retreating enemies and cautions against simple labels such as massacre/atrocity. Battle actions can be morally gray when tactically sound and in the context of war, especially with death-money mercenary practices.

The approved High Clade politics thread accepts that Drakhal coordinates the expensive opening attack to test Niall and Maya. The attack is not random banditry.

## Current Narrative Functions

- Plot function: explains how Drakhal can stage an expensive attack using expendable forces.
- Moral function: complicates Maya's first battle and Niall's response without forcing a single simple judgment.
- Class function: shows how the powerless are used by High Clade politics.
- Demi-human function: routes Durnai and possibly other visibly non-human or low-status people into marginal military/economic roles without making every mercenary demi-human.
- Exposition function: Harvick can explain mercenary tactics and costs in-scene.
- Character-pressure function: gives Maya a later benchmark for whether she is becoming more like Niall.

## Proposed Canon Core

If approved, the accepted thread would store:

- Mercenary forces in and around the Six Cities often recruit desperate poor people who accept lethal risk so their families receive death payments.
- Death money creates a grim economic logic: dying may help a family more than surviving crippled and unable to work.
- Mercenaries can be attackers, tools of High Clade politics, and exploited poor people at the same time.
- The opening Drakhal-backed attack uses forces who expect serious losses and try to avoid prolonged direct exposure to magi.
- Such forces may try to pull back, redirect, or disperse when a major magus arrives rather than standing in a fair fight.
- The attack's cost is itself suspicious; it implies serious backing and political purpose.
- Demi-human-descended people, especially Durnai and other marginalized low-status groups, can be common in mercenary/military roles, but mercenaries should not be treated as all demi-human or primarily defined by lineage.
- Moral language around Maya/Niall killing attacking or retreating mercenaries should stay complex and event-specific.

## Proposed Developmental Profile

Mercenary death money should make the social order feel predatory without turning individual mercenaries into saints.

The fighters attacking Rezin are still choosing or accepting violent work, and some may be brutal or dangerous. But the broader economy makes their choices narrow and ugly. High Clades can convert poverty into disposable bodies, then discuss the result through tactics, honor, and deterrence.

For Maya, this creates a wound and a mirror. Her first use of overwhelming power saves Rezin and horrifies her. Niall's continuation after the retreat shows one version of what tactical certainty can become. Later, Maya's own harder decisions can be compared against that moment without the text needing to decide everything for the reader.

## Continuity Constraints

- Do not make the opening attack random or cheap; it is Drakhal-backed and expensive.
- Do not make mercenaries morally identical to High Clade magi or political architects.
- Do not absolve attacking forces of violence just because many are exploited.
- Do not imply all mercenaries are demi-human.
- Preserve Durnai as plausibly common in military/mercenary roles.
- Preserve the tactical distinction between attacking, broken, retreating, wounded, and helpless fighters.
- Preserve that Maya's first-combat horror and later moral comparison both matter.
