# Decision 0020: Kinesis Terminology and Mechanics

Date: 2026-06-24

## Status

Accepted; stored in `bible/magic/magic-subversion.md`.

## Decision

The accepted name for the Archeon hereditary magic is `kinesis`. It can also be referred to as `Archeon kinesis` or `Archeon hereditary magic`.

`Subversion` is only Kelyra's misnomer for kinesis.

The accepted model is short-range, primarily kinetic redirection of energy. Kinesis can combine with other systems, such as Ink Pact movement, Shrouds, martial arts, anchors, tools, and technology, to allow more outward or aggressive manifestations.

The goal is always diversion because energy has to go somewhere. Redirection/diversion creates less backlash than trying to dead-stop or reverse energy.

The Shroud interaction remains candidate. Tremorsense or heightened sensitivity to kinetic manifestations remains candidate because it may be too confusing.

Antithesis is an Archeon rip-off of Zirene's older/prototypical designs, and Kelyra stole it. Exact Antithesis mechanics remain for object and technology review.

Serathis is a near-perfected Archeon-kinesis user, but her total power comes from mastery of the Shroud / martial arts / Ink Pact / kinesis stack. Kelyra and Davian working together surpass Serathis, and both have the potential to become better than her because of strong fundamental abilities combined with Kelyra's version of Archeon martial arts.

## Rationale

This keeps the accepted magic model focused and readable for Book 1 while preserving Kelyra's character-specific terminology. It also keeps broader Shroud and tremorsense rules out of accepted canon until they are needed.

## Consequences

- The durable magic record uses `Kinesis` as the display name.
- Character files may preserve `Subversion` as Kelyra's misnomer.
- Archeon records should refer to kinesis as the hereditary magic in the accepted stack.
- Antithesis/Zirene/gauntlet mechanics should be resolved in later object and technology reviews.
- Shroud interaction and tremorsense remain candidate.
